What is Consent Decision Making?

Beyond Consensus: A New Paradigm for Group Decisions

Consent Decision Making is a facilitated approach that fundamentally shifts how groups develop and decide on proposals. Unlike consensus (where everyone must agree) or majority voting (where some perspectives are overruled), consent operates on a different principle: a decision can move forward when no participant has a reasoned, substantial objection to it.

This approach distinguishes between:

  • Preferences ("I'd prefer a different approach")
  • Concerns ("I have worries but they don't block this decision")
  • Objections ("I see a specific reason why this proposal would harm our ability to achieve our aims")

The methodology creates space for all perspectives while maintaining forward momentum, focusing the group's intelligence on continuously improving proposals rather than debating positions.

The structured process ensures:

  • Every voice contributes meaningfully to proposal development
  • Objections are treated as valuable information for improvement
  • Decisions balance quality with practicality and timeliness
  • The group's collective wisdom shapes outcomes
  • Implementation benefits from broader ownership

Origins in Sociocracy

Consent Decision Making has its roots in Sociocracy, a governance system developed in the Netherlands by Kees Boeke and later refined by Gerard Endenburg. At its core, Sociocracy operates on the principle of consent rather than consensus or majority rule.

The approach has further evolved through Sociocracy 3.0 (S3), which expanded the original methodologies to make them more accessible and adaptable to various organizational contexts. S3 has helped spread consent-based decision making beyond traditional sociocratic organizations into businesses, non-profits, and communities worldwide.

While our facilitation draws on these rich traditions, we've adapted the process to be accessible and effective even for organizations with no prior experience in sociocratic methods.

This approach originated in sociocratic and holacratic governance systems but has evolved into a versatile decision-making methodology applicable across various organizational contexts.

When to Use Consent Decision Making

Transforming Decision Challenges Through Consent

Inclusive Dynamics recommends Consent Decision Making when your organization faces:

  • Complex Decisions With Diverse Stakeholders: When multiple perspectives need integration into coherent action
  • Recurring Decision Paralysis: When groups struggle to move from discussion to decision
  • Implementation Challenges: When decisions fail during execution due to lack of genuine buy-in
  • Power Imbalances in Decision Processes: When some voices consistently dominate while others remain unheard

  • Polarized Viewpoints: When groups are divided into opposing camps

Organizations typically see transformative results when using Consent Decision Making for policy development, strategic direction setting, resource allocation, process improvements, and other decisions where both quality and implementation depend on incorporating diverse perspectives.

The Consent Decision Making Process

A Structured Path to Better Decisions

The Consent Decision Making process follows a clear structure that transforms how groups develop and decide on proposals:

  1. Present the Proposal: The proposal is presented clearly and concisely
  2. Clarifying Questions: Participants ask questions solely to understand the proposal
  3. Reaction Round: Each person shares their initial thoughts without discussion or debate
  4. Amend & Clarify: The proposer may modify the proposal based on reactions
  5. Consent Round: Each person indicates whether they consent or object
  6. Address Objections: If objections arise, the group collaboratively integrates them
  7. Final Consent: The improved proposal is tested for consent again

This structured approach ensures all perspectives are heard while maintaining momentum toward decisions that work for the organization as a whole.
