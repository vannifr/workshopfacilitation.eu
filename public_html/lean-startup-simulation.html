Lean Startup simulation — test your idea in a day instead of planning it for a quarter

Most innovation projects fail slowly. The team spends months planning something nobody wants. The Lean Startup simulation compresses that cycle. Participants form teams, formulate hypotheses, design the cheapest possible test, run it, and get feedback — all in one day. They leave with a tested idea and a process they can repeat on their real work.

What is a Lean Startup simulation?

A Lean Startup simulation is a facilitated one-day workshop based on the Build-Measure-Learn loop. Teams work through a simplified innovation challenge in rapid cycles: form a hypothesis, design the smallest possible experiment, run it, measure the result, decide whether to continue or change direction.

The goal is not to solve the challenge — it's to learn the process. Teams leave knowing how to test an idea before investing in it, and how to stop a bad idea before it becomes a sunk cost.

When a Lean Startup simulation is not the right tool

  • When the team needs to solve an operational problem, not innovate — Lean Startup is for discovery, not optimisation
  • When leadership will not accept the learning from a failed hypothesis — the whole method depends on treating failure as information, not blame
  • When the decision about what to build has already been made and is non-negotiable — there is no point testing what cannot change

How we run Lean Startup simulations

  • We brief the team on the Build-Measure-Learn loop and why most innovation fails without it
  • We form teams and assign a challenge — sometimes fictional, sometimes drawn from real work
  • We run three or more rapid cycles with structured debriefs after each
  • We facilitate the pivot or persevere decision with concrete data, not opinion
  • We close by connecting the simulation experience to one real project the team is currently working on

Lean Startup simulation in practice

Product team: stopping the right project at the right time

A product team had been developing a feature for six months. After a Lean Startup simulation, they ran a proper hypothesis test on the feature's core assumption for the first time. The assumption was wrong. They stopped the feature, redirected the budget, and shipped something useful in eight weeks.

Innovation programme: from idea generation to tested concepts

An organisation ran an internal innovation programme that produced dozens of ideas and no working prototypes. We facilitated a two-day Lean Startup simulation at the start of the next cohort. Ten ideas entered. Three survived the testing phase with validated hypotheses. Those three became real projects.
